How the Assembly Actually Functions Day to Day

MLAs don't just vote on bills. Most of their power comes from questioning, challenging, and cornering the government through procedural tools. These are the weapons inside the chamber.

Question Hour — MLAs fire questions at ministers. 20–25 oral questions answered. Minister must answer on the spot or submit a written reply. Most watched part of the day.
Zero Hour — Unstructured. Any MLA can raise any urgent matter without prior notice. No formal rules — Speaker's discretion entirely. Often the most dramatic part.

Private Member Business — Non-minister MLAs can introduce their own bills or resolutions. Rarely becomes law, but important for putting issues on record.
❓
Question Hour
Daily · Most powerful accountability mechanism
Daily Tool
Three types
Starred (oral) — answer + supplementary questions allowed. Unstarred (written) — no follow-up. Short notice — less than 10 days' notice for urgent matters.
Why it matters
Ministers must answer personally, not delegate. A minister caught unprepared or giving a false answer faces instant public damage. Entire sessions can be shaped by one embarrassing Question Hour exchange.
TN context
TN Assembly sessions are notoriously short — sometimes only 10–15 days. This limits total Question Hours available — a common opposition grievance about accountability avoidance.
⚡
Adjournment Motion
Suspends all business to debate one urgent failure
Pressure Tool
What it does
Halts all Assembly business to immediately debate a specific recent failure of the government. Requires Speaker's admission — must show urgency and public importance.
Political effect
If admitted, it signals the Speaker has found the issue serious enough to override the government's legislative agenda. A major embarrassment for the ruling party.
Restriction
Cannot be used for matters already under court consideration. Most Adjournment Motions are rejected — admission itself is newsworthy.
💣
No-Confidence Motion
The nuclear option — brings down a government
Nuclear Option
How it works
Opposition moves that the Assembly has "no confidence" in the Council of Ministers. Requires support of at least 1/6th of total members (39 MLAs in TN) to be admitted.
If it passes
Government must resign immediately. No exceptions. The Governor then invites the next-largest group to form government, or dissolves the Assembly for fresh elections.
Floor Test
When the Governor doubts a government's majority (e.g., after defections), they can order a Floor Test — the CM must prove majority by a live vote in the Assembly within days.
The Whip System + Anti-Defection Law
—
One-Line Whip
Attend the session. You may vote as you wish. Rarely used except for procedural votes.
——
Two-Line Whip
Be present AND vote. Absence without permission is noticed. Semi-mandatory — breach is grounds for disciplinary action within party.
———
Three-Line Whip
Must be present. Must vote as directed. Used on confidence votes and major bills. Breaking a three-line whip = grounds for disqualification under Anti-Defection Law.
Anti-Defection Law (10th Schedule, 1985). If an MLA votes against their own party's three-line whip, or voluntarily gives up party membership, they lose their assembly seat — not just party membership. The Speaker decides disqualification. Exception: if ⅔ of the party's MLAs merge with another party, it's a legal split, not defection. This law was designed to stop horse-trading but the Speaker being a party member creates an inherent conflict of interest.
Chapter 14

High Court Writ Jurisdiction — The Citizen's Direct Weapon

You don't need to climb through lower courts. Any citizen can directly walk into the Madras High Court and challenge government action using a Writ Petition. These five writs are constitutional rights under Article 226. They are the most powerful tools a citizen has against the state.

Habeas Corpus
"You shall have the body"
Personal Liberty
What it challenges
Illegal detention or arrest. If anyone — police, state, or private person — is holding someone unlawfully, you file this. The court orders: produce the person in court NOW.
Who can file
Anyone — not just the detained person. A relative, friend, or even a stranger can file on their behalf. No formal relationship required. NGOs regularly file on behalf of victims.
Real use
Custodial disappearances, preventive detention (NSA, Goondas Act), illegal police lockup beyond 24 hours. The court can release the person immediately if detention is found illegal.
Mandamus
"We command"
Force Government to Act
What it challenges
Government inaction. When a public official refuses to do their legal duty — issue a certificate, process an application, release information — Mandamus orders them to do it.
Classic uses
Tahsildar refuses to issue community certificate. Corporation doesn't respond to RTI. Government officer sits on a valid license application. All of these can be mandamused.
Limitation
Cannot be used against a private party. Cannot force discretionary actions — only mandatory duties. The duty must be clearly defined in law.
Certiorari
"To be certified / to be informed"
Quash Bad Orders
What it challenges
Quash an illegal order already made. Used to cancel an order passed by a lower tribunal, quasi-judicial body, or government authority that acted without jurisdiction or violated natural justice.
Classic uses
Tax assessment made without hearing. Licence cancelled without giving reasons. Panchayat resolution passed illegally. University exam result invalidated without procedure. All can be quashed via Certiorari.
Often combined
Usually filed with Mandamus — "quash this order AND direct them to do it properly." This combination is the most common writ petition pattern in practice.
Prohibition
"To forbid"
Stop Ongoing Action
What it does
Stops a lower court or tribunal from proceeding when it has no jurisdiction or is about to exceed its powers. Unlike Certiorari (which cancels a completed order), Prohibition stops it mid-way.
When used
When a criminal court is taking up a matter it has no authority over. When a tribunal is about to rule outside its scope. Must be filed before the order is passed — it's preventive.
In practice
Less common than Certiorari. Mostly used in service law matters — preventing a disciplinary committee from completing an unfair inquiry. Time-sensitive by nature.
Quo Warranto
"By what authority?"
Challenge Illegal Appointment
What it challenges
Challenges someone's right to hold a public office. Filed when a person is appointed to or occupying a public position without legal authority or eligibility.
Classic uses
Person appointed as TNPSC member without qualifying criteria. Mayor/Councillor holding office despite disqualification. Vice Chancellor appointed by bypassing required procedure.
Who files
Any citizen — you don't need to be personally affected. It's a public interest tool. Frequently used to challenge university appointments and local body office-bearers.
How to Actually File a Writ Petition at Madras High Court
1
Identify the writ type
Is the government failing to act (Mandamus)? Detaining illegally (Habeas Corpus)? Making a bad order (Certiorari)?
2
Draft the petition with an advocate
Name the respondents (the official/body you're challenging), state the facts, quote the legal basis, and request specific relief.
3
File and get a number
Filed as "W.P. No. XXXX/20XX" at Madras HC or Madurai Bench. Court fee is nominal — typically ₹150–500.
4
Interim order (if urgent)
In genuine emergencies, the court can grant a Stay or Interim Order on the same day — temporarily halting the government's action until the full case is heard.
5
Notice to respondent + hearing
Government must file a counter-affidavit. Both sides argue. Judge decides — can quash, allow, or dismiss the petition.
Article 226 vs Article 32 — state vs Supreme Court writs. Under Article 226, the Madras High Court can issue writs against any authority — state, central, or private performing public function — within TN. Under Article 32, the Supreme Court can issue writs for Fundamental Rights violations anywhere in India. HC writs are broader (Article 226 covers rights beyond fundamental rights) but SC writs are the last resort and carry higher authority.

TNPSC & Civil Service — How the Bureaucracy Gets Built

Every certificate you've received, every scheme that got implemented, every road that got built — done by a civil servant. Here's how the state recruits, trains, and deploys its entire administrative machinery.

Two Separate Streams — All-India vs State Services
All-India Services
IAS · IPS · IFoS
Recruited by UPSC in New Delhi. Officers serve both Centre and states — posted to states by Centre after training. Tamil Nadu gets an annual cadre allocation. Key fact: The state government cannot transfer or punish an IAS officer without Centre's concurrence. This is a constant friction point.
Tamil Nadu State Services
TNCS · TNPS · Revenue Service
Recruited by TNPSC in Chennai. Entirely state cadre — state government controls postings, transfers, and disciplinary action. No central interference. These officers fill the vast majority of administrative posts in TN.

Parastatals & PSUs — The State's Operating Companies

The Tamil Nadu government doesn't just regulate — it directly runs companies. These parastatals implement policy, deliver services, and sometimes generate revenue. Understanding which body does what is essential to understanding who's really in charge of your electricity, bus, or house.

⚡
TANGEDCO
Tamil Nadu Generation and Distribution Corporation
MonopolyLoss-making
What it does
Generates power (thermal, hydro, wind), purchases from private and central generators, and distributes to all of TN's consumers. Monopoly distributor — no competition.
Financial reality
Accumulated losses of ~₹1.4 lakh crore. Free power subsidies (farmers, domestic) and below-cost tariffs are the reason. The state government periodically injects equity.
Governed by
Electricity Ministry. TNERC (TN Electricity Regulatory Commission) sets tariffs — technically independent of the government, though commissioners are appointed by it.
🚌
TNSTC + MTC
TN State Transport Corps (4 regional) + Metropolitan Transport Corp (Chennai)
5 corporationsSubsidised
What it does
Operates ~20,000+ buses connecting every district and taluk. Free travel for women, schoolchildren, disabled — these concessions are reimbursed by the state to the corporations.
Structure
5 regional TNSTC corps (Coimbatore, Kumbakonam, Salem, Villupuram, Tirunelveli) + MTC for Chennai. Each runs independently but under Transport Ministry.
Financial reality
All make operating losses — covered by state grants. Free schemes add ₹2,000+ Cr annual subsidy burden. Fleet aging is a chronic problem.
🍶
TASMAC
Tamil Nadu State Marketing Corporation — State liquor retail monopoly
Highly profitableMonopoly
What it does
Sole retailer of Indian Made Foreign Liquor (IMFL) in TN. ~6,800 outlets. Purchases from private manufacturers (United Spirits, United Breweries etc.) and retails at government-set prices.
Revenue machine
Contributes ~₹36,000 Cr to TN annually (excise duty + profit). Third largest revenue source after GST and own tax. Without TASMAC, the state budget has a serious problem.
Political tension
Every party campaigns for "prohibition" but governs with TASMAC intact. The fiscal dependency makes full prohibition nearly impossible without a dramatic alternative revenue source.
🏭
SIPCOT + TIDCO + TIDEL
Industrial estates, investment promotion, tech parks
InvestmentInfrastructure
SIPCOT
State Industries Promotion Corp — acquires land, develops industrial estates (Hosur, Sriperumbudur, Cheyyar), and allots plots to manufacturers. TN's industrial backbone.
TIDCO
TN Industrial Development Corp — takes equity stakes in joint ventures with private sector. Promotes new industries where private investment alone is insufficient.
TIDEL Park
IT-specific park in Chennai (India's first IT park, 2000). Managed by TIDEL — a joint venture of TIDCO and ELCOT. Model replicated in other cities.
🏠
TNHB + CMDA
TN Housing Board + Chennai Metropolitan Development Authority
PlanningHousing
TNHB
Tamil Nadu Housing Board — builds and allots affordable housing across the state. Slum clearance, EWS/LIG flats. Also develops commercial properties to cross-subsidise.
CMDA
Chennai Metropolitan Development Authority — the master planning body for Greater Chennai. Issues building plan approvals, controls land use, prepares the CMDA Master Plan (last revised 2026).
Key point
CMDA approval ≠ Corporation approval. Building in Chennai needs CMDA clearance for plan AND Corporation (CCMC) for construction and occupancy. Two separate bodies, two stages.
Chapter 17

Tribunals & Quasi-Judicial Bodies — Specialist Courts

Regular courts are slow and generalist. Tribunals are faster, specialist, and often the first stop for specific disputes — environment, service matters, consumer rights, real estate. They have judicial powers but aren't formal courts.

🌿
NGT — National Green Tribunal
Environmental disputes · National · Chennai bench
Handles environmental disputes, pollution cases, forest violations. Can impose fines, order clean-up, and shut down industries. Madras HC cannot hear environmental matters that NGT has jurisdiction over — it's exclusive. Appeals from NGT go directly to the Supreme Court.
TN cases: Sterlite Thoothukudi, Pallikaranai marshland encroachment, Cooum river pollution — all went through NGT.
🏢
CAT — Central Administrative Tribunal
Central govt service disputes · Chennai bench
For disputes involving central government employees — transfers, promotions, dismissals. State government employees go to the TN Administrative Tribunal instead. CAT Chennai bench handles all central services disputes for TN, Pondicherry, and Andaman.
Note: A central government employee fighting a wrongful transfer files in CAT, not Madras HC. But CAT orders can be challenged in HC.
🏠
TNRERA — Real Estate Regulatory Authority
Housing disputes · Under RERA Act 2016
Regulates builders and real estate agents in TN. Builders must register projects, disclose details, and hand over on time. If a builder delays or cheats, buyers file with TNRERA — which can impose penalties and order refunds. Far faster than civil courts.
Common use: Builder takes full payment, delays 3 years. Buyer files with TNRERA, gets interest on delay + penalty, without waiting 10 years in civil court.
👤
Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission
3 tiers: District · State · National
District Commission: Claims up to ₹50 lakh. State Commission (Chennai): ₹50L–₹2Cr, also appeals from District. NCDRC (Delhi): Above ₹2Cr and appeals from State. Free to file below ₹5 lakh. No lawyer required at district level.
Common use: Insurance company denies a valid medical claim. Hospital overcharges. Product defect. All go to Consumer Commission, not civil court.
⚖️
Labour Court + Industrial Tribunal
Worker disputes · Multiple locations in TN
Handles disputes between workers and employers — wrongful termination, wage disputes, strike legality. Labour Court for individual disputes. Industrial Tribunal for collective disputes (unions vs management). Government can refer disputes here; some disputes are mandatory referrals.
TN context: Tiruppur knitwear disputes, Chennai auto factory union matters — all go through these bodies before the High Court.
💧
Cauvery Water Disputes Tribunal
Inter-State · Special Constitutional body
Set up under the Inter-State River Waters Disputes Act. The 1991 Cauvery Water Disputes Tribunal gave TN 419 TMC feet. The Supreme Court's 2018 judgment finalised at 404.25 TMC. Cauvery disputes now go to the Cauvery Water Management Authority (CWMA) set up by the Centre in 2018.
Important: Once a river water dispute is referred to a tribunal, Madras HC cannot interfere — exclusive jurisdiction.
Tribunal vs Court — the key distinctions. Tribunals have technical expertise courts don't (environmental scientists on NGT bench, real estate experts in RERA). They are faster — no full CPC/CrPC procedure. But they have less independence — tribunal members are appointed by the executive, not judiciary, creating periodic bias concerns. All tribunal decisions can ultimately be appealed to the High Court or Supreme Court.
Chapter 18

Election Machinery — The ECI and How Elections Actually Run

Elections are the moment when the entire civic structure resets. The Election Commission of India (ECI) is a constitutional body that temporarily takes control of the entire civil service — police, collectors, everything — to ensure a free election.

How ECI Is Structured
Chief Election Commissioner
Appointed by President
Constitutionally protected — can only be removed like a Supreme Court judge (impeachment by Parliament). Heads the Commission. Decisions made collectively with Election Commissioners.
Election Commissioners (2)
Appointed by President
The CEC cannot act alone — majority vote needed. Since 2023, a selection committee (PM, Opposition leader, Cabinet minister) nominates commissioners — reforming earlier executive-only control.
Chief Electoral Officer (TN)
Senior IAS officer · TN
State-level ECI representative. Coordinates the District Election Officers (Collectors). During elections, these officers report to ECI — not the state government.
Model Code of Conduct — What Kicks In When
When
What the MCC prohibits
Day 1 — Schedule announced
Immediate effect. Government cannot announce new schemes, lay foundation stones for projects, or make policy promises that could influence voters.
Throughout election period
No government advertisements. No use of government machinery for campaigning. Civil servants cannot be transferred without ECI clearance. All major postings need ECI approval.
48 hrs before polling
Silence period. No campaigning of any kind — meetings, rallies, advertisements. Liquor sales banned. Media cannot publish exit polls.
Polling day
Central forces deployed. District Collectors become Returning Officers. Police under ECI control, not state home ministry. Booth-level officers verify rolls.
Post-result
MCC lifted. Government resumes normal functioning. Pending schemes, transfers, and announcements can proceed.
Delimitation — why constituency boundaries matter. Delimitation Commissions (set up by Parliament) redraw constituency boundaries based on Census data. The last delimitation used the 1971 Census — frozen since 1977 to prevent states that controlled population (like TN) from losing seats. The next delimitation after the 2026 Census is expected by 2031, and will likely reduce TN's representation in Lok Sabha relative to high-population states. This is TN's biggest long-term political concern about federalism.
What the Election Commission can do that surprises people. ECI can transfer any state government officer if it believes they are compromising election integrity. ECI can recognise or de-recognise political parties and assign/withdraw election symbols. ECI adjudicates on party splits — in the famous 2022 Maharashtra case, ECI had to decide which faction of Shiv Sena was the "real" party and got the bow-and-arrow symbol. Its decisions here are final (subject only to SC appeal).

Union Budget & TN State Budget — Full Anatomy

Two separate budget cycles. Same country, very different structures, constraints, and players. Understanding both — and how they connect — is what makes the money make sense.

The Union Budget is not one document — it is six. The Annual Financial Statement (the real budget), the Finance Bill (tax changes), the Demands for Grants (department-wise spending), the Macro-Economic Framework Statement, the Fiscal Policy Strategy Statement, and the Medium Term Fiscal Policy Statement. When the FM opens the briefcase, all six land in Parliament simultaneously.

Vote on Account — The Emergency Budget Mechanism
In an election year, the outgoing government cannot present a full budget (would amount to using public money to campaign). Instead, Parliament passes a Vote on Account — authorising spending for 2 months (sometimes extended to 4) to keep government running. The new government then presents a full budget after elections. TN 2026: exactly this scenario — the outgoing DMK government would have presented a Vote on Account, and the new government presents the full TN budget after May 2026.

TN State Budget — The Separate Cycle Running in Parallel
Key difference from Union: State governments cannot print money or borrow from RBI directly. They borrow from markets, NSSF (National Small Savings Fund), and institutional lenders (World Bank, ADB) — all subject to the Centre's approval. The Centre can tighten or loosen state borrowing limits as fiscal pressure management. This is a real lever over states.

The Three Deficits — Fully Explained
Revenue Deficit = Revenue Expenditure − Revenue Receipts
If positive: the government is borrowing to pay daily running costs — salaries, subsidies. This is the most dangerous deficit. It means current operations are unsustainable on current income. A revenue surplus state can fund capital works without borrowing.
TN FY25: Revenue Deficit ~₹35,000 Cr — meaning TN is borrowing just to meet routine expenses, not to build anything. This has been a chronic problem since 2015.
Fiscal Deficit = Total Expenditure − Total Receipts excl. Borrowings
The total borrowing requirement. The number that fills every newspaper on budget day. Includes both revenue and capital deficits. Capped at 3% of GSDP under FRBM — states exceeding this need Centre's special permission.
TN FY25: Fiscal Deficit ~₹85,000 Cr (~3.2% of GSDP). Marginally above the 3% limit. TN regularly negotiates flexibility with the Centre's DEA (Dept of Economic Affairs).
Primary Deficit = Fiscal Deficit − Interest Payments
The "true" new borrowing — stripping out what you're borrowing just to service old debt. If Primary Deficit = 0, you've stabilised debt levels even if fiscal deficit is high. Economists watch this more than fiscal deficit.
TN FY25: Fiscal Deficit ₹85K Cr − Interest ₹55K Cr = Primary Deficit ~₹30K Cr. TN is still adding real new debt, not just rolling over old debt.
FRBM Act — The Fiscal Rules That Govern State Borrowing
3%
Fiscal Deficit Cap
States cannot borrow beyond 3% of GSDP without Centre's approval. TN repeatedly approaches or exceeds this. Post-COVID, 4–4.5% was allowed temporarily.
0%
Revenue Deficit Target
FRBM requires states to eliminate revenue deficits. TN has failed this for years. The 15th FC set conditions on grants tied to revenue deficit reduction.
+0.5%
Borrowing Headroom
States get extra 0.5% GSDP borrowing if they undertake power sector reforms (TANGEDCO). TN has partially unlocked this via TANGEDCO restructuring.

The GST Council — the body that changed everything. Post-GST (2017), neither Union nor states can unilaterally change most indirect tax rates. Both sit on the GST Council (Union Finance Minister chairs, all state FMs are members). Decisions need ¾ majority. This is unprecedented cooperative federalism — but also means TN cannot reduce GST on, say, textiles without Centre's approval. Simultaneously, the Centre cannot raise GST without state consent. A genuine shared power over India's largest tax base.
