The 7th Schedule — Who Can Make Law on What

India's Constitution divides all possible law-making subjects into three lists. This is the rulebook. Without understanding it, every Centre–State conflict is just noise. Parliament and State Assemblies cannot stray outside their assigned lists.

Union List
97 subjects · Only Parliament
Defence & Armed Forces
Foreign Affairs & Treaties
Atomic Energy & Space
Railways & National Highways
Banking & Insurance
Currency & Reserve Bank
Income Tax & Customs
Inter-State Trade
Citizenship & Naturalisation
Concurrent List
47 subjects · Both, Union wins conflict
Education (moved here in 1976)
Forests & Wildlife
Electricity
Labour & Trade Unions
Criminal Law (IPC, CrPC)
Marriage & Divorce
Drugs & Poisons
Stamp Duties (rates)
Price Control
State List
66 subjects · Only State Legislature

What happens when there's a conflict on the Concurrent List?
State passes a law
e.g. TN education fee regulation
⚡
Parliament passes a law
on the same subject
→
Parliament law wins
State law is void
One exception: If the state law received the President's assent before Parliament passed a conflicting law, the state law survives in that state. This is why states sometimes rush contentious laws to the Governor/President for assent.
Residuary Powers go to Parliament — not states.
Anything not in any of the three lists? Parliament decides. This is the opposite of the USA, where states hold residuary powers. India's framers deliberately made the Centre stronger.
Why was Education moved to Concurrent in 1976?
Originally a state subject. Indira Gandhi's government moved it to the Concurrent List during the Emergency, allowing the Centre to set national education standards. TN's NEET resistance is a direct consequence of this.
Chapter 08

Central Investigative Agencies Operating in Tamil Nadu

These agencies are created by Parliament and answer to the Union government — not the state. Understanding each agency's jurisdiction, consent requirements, and special powers is what separates real comprehension from headline reading.

🔍
CBI
Central Bureau of Investigation · Under MHA / DSPE Act
Needs Consent Corruption + Serious Crime
State Consent Rule
YES — general consent required. TN can withdraw it. When withdrawn, CBI needs a court order (usually HC or SC) to investigate within TN.
Special Power
Can take over cases from state police. Courts can direct CBI transfer against a state's wishes — a powerful judicial tool.
Used For
Corruption by public servants, serious fraud, inter-state crime, cases where state police are seen as compromised.
💰
ED
Enforcement Directorate · Under Finance Ministry · PMLA + FEMA
No Consent Needed Reversed Burden
State Consent Rule
NONE needed. ED can investigate in any state without permission. State governments cannot block ED operations.
The PMLA Reversal
Accused must prove innocence — not ED proving guilt. Can provisionally attach property before any conviction. Bail is extremely hard to obtain.
Why It Matters
PMLA's reversed burden + property attachment + no consent = the Centre's most potent lever over state politicians. Opposition-ruled states experience this acutely.
📊
Income Tax (CBDT)
Central Board of Direct Taxes · Under Finance Ministry
No Consent Tax Evasion
State Consent Rule
None needed. IT raids can occur anywhere, anytime, on any taxpayer — individual or organisation.
Special Power
Survey and search powers — can enter premises, seize documents and cash, freeze bank accounts pending assessment.
Political Use
IT raids on political donors or media houses ahead of elections are a frequently observed pressure tool, regardless of which party is in power at Centre.
🛡
NIA
National Investigation Agency · Under MHA · NIA Act 2008
No Consent Terrorism Only
State Consent Rule
Zero consent needed. NIA can take over any terrorism, secessionist, or UAPA case from state police automatically.
Special Court
NIA cases go to designated Special NIA Courts, not regular sessions courts — faster, different evidentiary standards.
TN Relevance
LTTE-linked cases, ISIS-radicalization cases, and smuggling with terror nexus have been taken up by NIA in TN without state consent.
💊
NCB
Narcotics Control Bureau · Under MHA · NDPS Act
No Consent NDPS Reversed Burden
State Consent Rule
None needed. Works alongside TN's own state drug enforcement — jurisdiction overlaps frequently.
NDPS Act
Like PMLA, NDPS has strict bail provisions — courts must record reasons in writing to grant bail. Reversed burden for commercial quantity.
TN Relevance
Coastal drug trafficking (Sri Lanka-TN route), ganja cultivation in border districts — NCB coordinates with state police.

Chapter 09

Revenue Administration — The Chain Most Citizens Actually Use

The police and judiciary get all the attention — but for most citizens, the government they interact with daily is the revenue administration. Land records, caste certificates, income certificates — all flow through this chain.

1
District Collector
IAS Officer · Head of District Administration
The single most powerful officer in a district. Wears three hats simultaneously: Revenue Officer (land administration), District Magistrate (law & order), and Electoral Officer (elections). All government schemes in the district answer to the Collector.
Land acquisition Disaster relief Supervises all depts Election authority
2
Revenue Divisional Officer (RDO)
Sub-Divisional Level · Usually a Deputy Collector (IAS/IRS)
Each district is divided into sub-divisions. The RDO is the Collector's representative at this level — handles land disputes, issues community certificates, oversees Tahsildars below them, and has quasi-judicial powers for land matters.
Community certificate appeals Land dispute orders Oversees Tahsildars
3
Tahsildar
Taluk Level · Tamil Nadu Revenue Service Officer
The face of revenue administration for most citizens. This is where you go for caste certificate, income certificate, nativity certificate, and land patta issues. Tahsildars have wide powers — they can certify, attach, and record land ownership.
Caste certificate Income certificate Land patta Nativity certificate Encumbrance certificate
4
Revenue Inspector (RI)
Firka Level · Group of Villages
Oversees a cluster of villages (a firka). Inspects land, verifies agricultural data, supervises VAOs, and is the first official to physically visit disputed land. Crucial link between the village and the taluk office.
Field verification of land Agricultural survey Supervises VAOs
5
Village Administrative Officer (VAO)
Village Level · One per revenue village
The most ground-level revenue official. Maintains the village land register (A-register), records crop details (chitta/adangal), helps citizens apply for certificates, and is often the first government contact for any village resident. Historically a powerful local figure.
A-register (land records) Chitta / Adangal Patta applications Scheme beneficiary lists Certificate initiation
The documents that change lives — and where they come from:
Patta — Land ownership record. Issued by Tahsildar.
Chitta — Who holds the land (possession). Maintained by VAO.
Adangal — What crops are grown on the land. Maintained by VAO.
EC (Encumbrance Certificate) — Confirms no loans/mortgages on a property. Critical for buying land. From Sub-Registrar office.
Community Certificate — Caste/tribe classification for reservations. Issued by Tahsildar, verified by RDO.
Income Certificate — Annual family income, for scholarships and schemes. Issued by Tahsildar.
Chapter 10

Accountability — The Watchdogs Over Government

Every power structure needs a check. These bodies exist to audit, expose, investigate, and give citizens recourse against the government — without them, everything covered so far operates unchecked.

🔬
CAG — Comptroller & Auditor General
Constitutional Body · Article 148 · Independent of both Centre and State
Audits every rupee spent by the government — Union, state, and government-owned companies. The CAG cannot be removed by the government (only by Parliament, like a Supreme Court judge). After auditing, the CAG submits a report to the Legislature — called the CAG Report — which is then examined by the Public Accounts Committee of the Assembly.
In practice: When the CAG flags ₹2,000 Cr worth of irregularities in a TASMAC tender, that report becomes a political weapon for the opposition — and can trigger further investigation.
📄
RTI — Right to Information Act, 2005
Citizen's Tool · Any citizen can use it · Free for BPL
Any citizen can ask any government department for any information — and the department must reply within 30 days. If they don't, or if you're unhappy, you can appeal to the State Information Commission (SIC) or Central IC. This is arguably the single most powerful transparency tool a citizen has.
Exemptions: National security, cabinet deliberations, personal privacy, information given in confidence. In practice: RTI is used to expose ghost beneficiaries in welfare schemes, fake construction bills, and officer misconduct.
⚖️
Tamil Nadu Vigilance Commission
State Anti-Corruption Body · Advises on disciplinary proceedings
Deals with corruption allegations against state government employees. The Directorate of Vigilance and Anti-Corruption (DVAC) is TN's investigative arm — like a state-level CBI for corruption. DVAC files cases in special anti-corruption courts. Unlike the Lokayukta (which TN doesn't have in a strong form), DVAC works through the criminal courts system.
Key gap: TN does not have a strong independent Lokayukta. Politicians and senior officers are largely outside DVAC's reach — it mainly catches lower-level corruption.
🏛
TN Human Rights Commission
Quasi-Judicial Body · State-level NHRC equivalent
Investigates complaints of human rights violations by state government officers — police brutality, custodial deaths, denial of basic rights. Can recommend compensation and action. Cannot try criminal cases, but its findings can trigger FIRs and departmental action.
In practice: Custodial death in a TN police station → family files with TNHRC → Commission issues notice to SP → inquiry ordered → compensation recommended.
👩‍⚖️
TN State Women's Commission
Statutory Body · Recommends, mediates, investigates
Investigates complaints of harassment, discrimination, and violence against women involving government officials or public spaces. Can summon officers, recommend action, and refer cases to police. Does not have the power to punish directly.
Important distinction: The Commission is an advisory body — it cannot convict. It creates pressure and documentation that feeds into actual police or court proceedings.
📢
Public Accounts Committee (PAC)
Legislative Committee · MLAs examining government spending
A committee of MLAs that scrutinises the CAG's audit reports. The PAC calls government officials before it, questions spending decisions, and submits its own report to the Assembly. It is the Assembly's primary financial oversight mechanism over the executive.
The chain: CAG audits → finds irregularity → reports to Assembly → PAC examines → summons officials → submits findings → Assembly debates → government must respond.

Governor's Special Powers & Article 356

Most of the time, the Governor is ceremonial. But in specific situations — a hung assembly, a constitutional crisis, or a politically motivated Centre — the Governor becomes the most consequential figure in the state. Understanding the rules and safeguards here is essential.

Normal Powers — Must act on CM's advice
Appoints CM (must be leader of majority)
Gives assent to bills — cannot refuse indefinitely
Summons, prorogues, and dissolves Assembly — on CM's advice
Addresses the Assembly
Appoints Advocate General, TNPSC members
Discretionary Powers — Can act independently
Choosing CM in a hung assembly (no majority)
Dismissing CM if they lose floor majority and refuse to resign
Dissolving Assembly if no stable govt is possible
Reserving a bill for President's consideration instead of giving assent
Sending a report to Centre recommending Article 356

Article 356 — President's Rule: How It Works

Article 356 allows the Centre to dismiss a state government and take direct control. It is the most extreme use of central power. The Sarkaria Commission (1988) and the S.R. Bommai case (1994) created crucial safeguards.

1
Governor sends a report to President
Stating that the state's constitutional machinery has broken down — either because no party has a majority, or the government is violating the Constitution.
2
Union Cabinet recommends to President
The Centre (PM + Cabinet) recommends proclamation of President's Rule. The President acts on this advice.
3
President's Rule Proclaimed
State government dismissed. Governor becomes executive head. State Assembly is suspended or dissolved. Parliament controls state legislation.
4
Bommai Safeguard — Parliamentary Approval Within 2 Months
The S.R. Bommai judgment (1994) ruled: the proclamation must be approved by both Houses of Parliament within 2 months. Until then, the Assembly cannot be dissolved — only suspended. Courts can also review whether the proclamation was valid.
The "Bill Pocket Veto" problem.
Governors can simply sit on bills — not give assent, not return them, not reserve them. This is called the de facto pocket veto. TN's Electricity Regulation Bill and other bills have sat unsigned for months/years during Centre-State tensions. The Constitution gives no time limit for assent, creating this gap.
Hung Assembly — who becomes CM?
Governor invites the single largest party first. If they can't prove majority in a floor test within a set timeframe, the second-largest coalition gets a chance. The Governor has real, consequential discretion here — and political bias has been alleged in Maharashtra (2019) and other states.
Chapter 12 · Deep Dive

The Budget Cycle — How Government Money Actually Works

The budget is not a spreadsheet. It is the government's statement of intent — what it will tax, what it will borrow, and what it will spend. Understanding the full cycle from preparation to Finance Commission devolution is essential to understanding how anything gets funded.

The Mental Model — Think of It Like a Household

Revenue Account
= Monthly salary & regular bills
Regular income (taxes, fees) vs regular spending (salaries, subsidies, interest on old loans). If spending > income here, it's a Revenue Deficit — you're borrowing to pay monthly bills. Deeply problematic.
Capital Account
= Home loan & investments
Borrowing for long-term assets (roads, hospitals). Unlike a revenue deficit, a capital deficit is acceptable — as long as the asset being created justifies the borrowing.
Fiscal Deficit
= Total credit card bill for the year
Total expenditure minus all receipts except borrowings. The entire gap the government fills by borrowing. FRBM Act targets this at ≤3% of GDP/GSDP. TN typically runs 3–4%.
Primary Deficit
= New borrowing, excluding old debt service
Fiscal Deficit minus interest payments. If Primary Deficit = 0, you are only borrowing to pay interest on past loans — not taking on new debt. A better measure of underlying fiscal health.

The Three Funds — Where Government Money Actually Lives

🏦
Consolidated Fund of India / State
All revenues + borrowings flow in · All payments flow out
Every rupee of tax, fee, and borrowing must enter here. Every government payment must come from here. Parliament/Assembly must vote to authorize every single withdrawal — this is the budget vote. Nothing can bypass this without legislative approval.
🚨
Contingency Fund
Union: ₹500 Cr · States: smaller corpus
An imprest account for unforeseen emergencies — floods, sudden crises — where waiting for Parliament is impossible. The President (or Governor for states) can authorise spending. Must be replenished by Parliament at the next session.
📦
Public Account
Money held in trust — not the government's own
Money the government holds for others — PPF deposits, provident fund contributions, small savings, court deposits. The government is a banker here, not a spender. No Parliamentary vote needed because it's not the government's money to spend.

Finance Commission — How the Centre Shares Tax With States

Every 5 years, the President appoints a Finance Commission to decide: (1) what % of Union taxes goes to all states (vertical devolution), and (2) how that share is split between states (horizontal devolution). The 15th Finance Commission covers 2021–2026.

15th Finance Commission — How TN's 4.189% Share is Calculated
Income Distance (45%)
Population (15%)
Area (15%)
Demog. Perf. (12.5%)
Forest (10%)
Tax (2.5%)
Income Distance 45% — states with lower per-capita income get more
Population 15% — 2011 census used
Area 15% — larger states get more
Demographic Performance 12.5% — reward for controlling population growth (TN benefits)
Forest & Ecology 10% — forest cover maintained
Tax Effort 2.5% — reward for better tax collection
Why TN gets only 4.189% — despite being industrially advanced. TN's high per-capita income means it scores low on the Income Distance factor (45% weight). Poorer states like UP and Bihar get more because they have greater "distance" from the national average income.
TN's win: Demographic Performance. TN controlled its fertility rate early (TFR ~1.7 vs national average ~2.0). This factor rewards exactly that. Without it, TN's share would be even lower. The irony: reducing population means fewer votes in the population-weighted formula, but gains back partly via this factor.
Vertical Devolution: 41% of divisible pool to states (down from 42% — 1% was adjusted for J&K/Ladakh becoming UTs). The divisible pool = Union's tax revenues excluding certain cesses and surcharges. Note: PMGKY-type cesses are outside the divisible pool — states do not share in those collections, which is a point of ongoing political friction.
