Chapter 01

India is a Federation — Three Layers of Government

India is not governed by just one government. Powers are split across three tiers. Each tier has its own elected body, its own powers, and its own responsibilities. Tamil Nadu is a state — it sits in the middle tier.

Tier 1 🏛 Union Government
Parliament (Lok Sabha + Rajya Sabha) President of India Prime Minister + Union Cabinet Supreme Court
Controls: Defence · Foreign Affairs · Railways · Currency · Income Tax · National Highways
Tier 2 🟠 State Government ← TN is here
Legislative Assembly (Vidhan Sabha) Governor Chief Minister + Cabinet High Court
Controls: Police · State roads · Agriculture · Public health · Education · State taxes
Tier 3 🟢 Local Government
Municipal Corporations Municipalities Panchayats
Controls: Local roads · Water supply · Sanitation · Building permits · Street lights
The 73rd & 74th Amendments (1992) made local government a constitutional right — ensuring that villages and cities have their own elected representatives, not just bureaucrats. This is why you have a Panchayat President in every village and a Councillor in every ward.

  • Represents the permanent (non-political) government

Civil vs Political: Politicians come and go with elections. IAS officers stay — giving continuity to administration regardless of which party is in power.

How Elections Work — FPTP System

01
One Constituency
TN is divided into 234 single-member constituencies. Each elects exactly one MLA.
02
You Vote Once
Each voter in a constituency casts one vote for one candidate (MLA). You are not voting for the CM directly.
03
First Past the Post
The candidate with the most votes — even if not 50%+ — wins the seat. No runoffs.
04
Majority → CM
The party winning 118+ seats forms the government. Their leader is invited by the Governor to be CM.

Key Roles Inside the Assembly

🔨 Speaker
Elected by all 234 MLAs. Runs Assembly sessions — decides who speaks, maintains order, and has the casting vote if there's a tie. Independent of party politics once elected.
👤 MLA (Member of Legislative Assembly)
Elected representative of one constituency. Their job: raise issues affecting their area, vote on bills, question ministers, attend sessions, and represent ~2 lakh voters.
⚔️ Leader of the Opposition
Leader of the largest party not in government. Critiques the ruling government, holds it accountable, and presents an alternative viewpoint on every major policy.
🎙️ Leader of the House
The Chief Minister. Leads the ruling side in the Assembly. Presents government policies, answers questions, and manages floor proceedings for the ruling party.
📋 Chief Whip
Party's disciplinarian inside the Assembly. Issues "whip" orders to MLAs — telling them how to vote on a bill. Breaking a party whip can result in disqualification.
🏛 Assembly Secretariat
The permanent staff of the Assembly — maintains records, drafts bills, sends notices, and ensures the institution runs regardless of which party is in power.

Local Governance — Closest to the People

Below the state government is the third tier — local bodies. These handle everyday civic life: your road, your water supply, your dustbin, your streetlight. TN has two parallel systems: one for urban areas, one for rural.

🏙️ Urban Track Cities & Towns
Level 1 — Largest
Municipal Corporation
15 corporations · incl. Chennai, Coimbatore, Madurai
For large cities. Has a Mayor, elected Councillors per ward, and a Commissioner (IAS) who runs day-to-day operations.
Level 2
Municipality
121 municipalities across TN
For medium-sized towns. Has a Chairman (elected by Councillors) and an Executive Officer (state-appointed).
Level 3 — Smallest Urban
Town Panchayat
528 town panchayats
For semi-urban areas transitioning from village to town. Has a President and Ward Members, directly elected.
🌾 Rural Track Villages & Blocks
Level 1 — Largest
District
38 districts in Tamil Nadu
Administered by a District Collector (IAS). The single most powerful officer at ground level — oversees all state programmes in the district.
Level 2 — Block/Taluk
Panchayat Union
385 panchayat unions
Covers a group of villages. Managed by a Block Development Officer (BDO) and an elected Panchayat Union Council with a Chairman/Chairperson.
Level 3 — Smallest
Village Panchayat
12,618 village panchayats
The base of democracy — one per village (or cluster). Directly elected Panchayat President and Ward Members handle local roads, drains, and schemes.

Who is Who — Local Body Roles Explained

👑
Mayor
Municipal Corporation · Political Head
Elected by Councillors from among themselves (in TN). Presides over council meetings, represents the city ceremonially. The Commissioner (IAS) is actually the operational head running the city day-to-day.
🗳
Councillor
Municipal Corporation/Municipality · Ward Rep
Directly elected by residents of a ward (a subdivision of a city). Their job: represent the ward's needs in the corporation council, ensure roads/water/drainage in their ward are maintained.
🌾
Panchayat President
Village Panchayat · Directly Elected
Directly elected by village residents. Chairs the Village Panchayat and is the first point of contact between the state and the villager. Implements welfare schemes and oversees village infrastructure.
📋
District Collector
District · IAS Officer
An IAS officer appointed by the state. The most powerful government official in a district — wears multiple hats: executive head (development), District Magistrate (law & order), and electoral officer during elections.
MLA vs. Councillor vs. MP — what's the difference?
MP (Member of Parliament) → represents a constituency at the national level in Lok Sabha, deals with national laws.
MLA (Member of Legislative Assembly) → represents a constituency at the state level, deals with state laws.
Councillor → represents a ward in a city/town, deals with purely local civic issues — roads, drains, waste.
Chapter 05

From Idea to Act — How a Bill Becomes Law

A "bill" is a proposed law. It has to pass through several formal stages before it becomes an "Act" (law). In Tamil Nadu, since there's only one house, it's a relatively straightforward process — but the Governor's assent is the final gate.

01
💡
Introduction
A minister or MLA introduces the bill. The text is circulated to all members. No debate at this stage.
02
📖
First Reading
The bill's title and purpose are read aloud. No debate. Assembly acknowledges the bill exists.
03
🗣
Second Reading
Main debate stage. MLAs discuss the bill's principles, raise objections, and propose amendments clause by clause.
04
🔍
Committee Stage
Optionally referred to a Select Committee for detailed review. Committee may call experts and submit recommendations.
05
🗳
Third Reading & Vote
Final debate. Assembly votes. A simple majority (50%+1 of members present) passes the bill.
06
🏛
Governor's Assent
Bill sent to Governor. Can: ✅ Assent · ↩️ Return for reconsideration · 🔼 Reserve for President
07
⚖️
Gazette Notification → ACT
Published in the Tamil Nadu Government Gazette. Now it's law. Binding on all citizens.
What if the Governor returns a bill?
The Assembly can simply pass the bill again — exactly as it was — and the Governor must give assent the second time. The Governor cannot veto indefinitely, but can delay.
Money Bills are special.
Bills involving taxation or government spending (Money Bills) can only be introduced by a Minister — not a private MLA. They follow the same path but cannot be amended by anyone other than the government.
Chapter 06

Law & Order — The Police and the Courts

Law and order in Tamil Nadu works through two independent systems: the Police (enforces laws) and the Courts (interprets and applies laws). Both operate through a strict hierarchy.

Tamil Nadu Police — Rank Hierarchy

Reading top to bottom: most senior → ground level. IPS officers hold ranks from DGP down to DSP. Below that are State Police Service officers.

Director General of Police (DGP) Head of TN Police · IPS
Additional Director General (ADGP) Heads wings: CID, Special, Traffic · IPS
Inspector General of Police (IGP) Oversees a zone / multiple districts · IPS
Deputy Inspector General (DIG) Supervises a range of districts · IPS
Superintendent of Police (SP) / Commissioner Controls one district / city commissionerate · IPS
Deputy SP (DSP) / Asst. Commissioner (ACP) Sub-division level · State Police Service
Inspector of Police Heads a police station · Station House Officer
Sub-Inspector (SI) First-response officer · handles FIRs
ASI → Head Constable → Constable The boots on the ground
District System: Outside major cities, a Superintendent of Police (SP) heads all police in one district. Under them: DSPs for each sub-division, Inspectors for each station.
Commissionerate System: In major cities (Chennai, Coimbatore, Madurai, Trichy, Salem, Tirunelveli), a Commissioner of Police (IPS) heads city police, with DCPs and ACPs below them.
FIR → Station: Every complaint starts with an FIR (First Information Report) at a police station, headed by an Inspector. They investigate, and if serious, refer to higher ranks.
Home Ministry link: The entire police force answers to the Home Ministry at the state level. The CM (via the Home Ministry) controls all police postings and operations.

Courts are independent of the government — judges cannot be removed by politicians. Cases travel upwards through appeals.

Police vs. Courts — different branches, different jobs. Police are part of the executive — they implement the law and report to the government. Courts are the judiciary — they interpret the law and are constitutionally independent. A police officer cannot tell a judge what to rule, and a judge cannot tell a police officer where to patrol.

The 7th Schedule — Who Can Make Law on What

The Constitution divides all of governance into three lists. Before asking "why isn't the Centre doing something about this?" or "why is the state not acting?" — the answer is almost always in the 7th Schedule. This is the rulebook for the entire federal system.

Conflict rule: If a state law and a central law both cover the same subject on the Concurrent List, the Central law automatically prevails — even if the state law came first. The state law is not "illegal" but it becomes inoperative to the extent of inconsistency.
Residuary powers: Anything not explicitly in any of the three lists? Parliament gets it. That's why Parliament could legislate on cyberspace, genetic engineering, and cryptocurrencies — none existed when the Constitution was written.
Chapter 08

Central Investigative Agencies — The Centre's Long Arm

These are not state police. They are central bodies with their own laws, their own jurisdiction, and their own relationship with state governments. The key question for each: does the agency need the state's permission to operate within TN?

CBI
Central Bureau of Investigation
State consent: REQUIRED
Under Ministry of Personnel (effectively PMO oversight)
Law Delhi Special Police Establishment Act, 1946 — not a statutory body, created by executive resolution
Investigates Corruption in central government, serious fraud, court-transferred cases
The consent rule: CBI needs a state's "general consent" under Section 6 to operate. TN withdrew this in 2021. Post-withdrawal, CBI must get a specific court order for every investigation — High Court or Supreme Court must transfer the case. It still can investigate central government employees without consent.
ED
Enforcement Directorate
State consent: NOT NEEDED
Under Ministry of Finance — Department of Revenue
Laws PMLA (Prevention of Money Laundering Act) · FEMA (Foreign Exchange Management Act)
Investigates Proceeds of crime, money laundering, forex violations
Why ED is the most feared agency right now: Under PMLA Section 24, the burden of proof is reversed — the accused must prove the property is NOT proceeds of crime. ED can attach property before any conviction. Bail is governed by a "twin test" (Section 45) — court must believe you're not guilty AND won't re-offend. Both conditions simultaneously = nearly impossible to satisfy. No state's consent can stop ED from entering.
Income Tax Dept.
CBDT — Central Board of Direct Taxes
State consent: NOT NEEDED
Under Ministry of Finance
Law Income Tax Act, 1961 — Black Money Act, Benami Transactions Act
Investigates Tax evasion, undisclosed income, benami properties
Raids (Search & Seizure): Can enter premises without prior notice under Section 132. Seizes cash, documents, digital records. Different from ED — IT deals with tax owed to government, ED deals with criminal money laundering. But cases often start with an IT raid and then get referred to ED.
NCB
Narcotics Control Bureau
State consent: NOT NEEDED
Under Ministry of Home Affairs
Law NDPS Act (Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985)
Investigates Drug trafficking, narcotics networks, international drug flows
Joint operations: Usually works with state police, Customs, and Coast Guard. NDPS has harsh bail conditions similar to PMLA — hard to get bail for drug offenses above a threshold quantity. Jurisdiction overlaps with state police on local drug cases.
NIA
National Investigation Agency
State consent: NOT NEEDED — can take over from state police
Under Ministry of Home Affairs · Formed post-26/11 attacks in 2008
Law NIA Act, 2008 — can investigate Unlawful Activities Prevention Act (UAPA) cases, terrorism, human trafficking, counterfeit currency
Unlike CBI: NIA does not need state consent and can take over a case from state police suo motu (on its own) if it involves terrorism or national security. Has its own Special NIA Courts. UAPA (like PMLA) makes bail extremely difficult — can hold a person for up to 180 days without chargesheet.
The combined pressure system — how it works in practice: When the Centre and a state government are in conflict, the toolkit is: Governor stalling bills or sending adverse reports → ED attaching assets of party donors or politicians under PMLA → IT raids on party financiers → CBI getting court orders to investigate → NIA invoking national security if any angle exists. These are used as individual actions legally, but together they constitute systemic political pressure. Understanding this is essential to reading current-affairs governance news.
Chapter 09

Revenue Administration — The Chain You Actually Interact With

Most citizens never meet an IPS officer or walk into the High Court. But everyone eventually needs a caste certificate, or a land record, or income proof. That entire chain is the Revenue Administration — a parallel hierarchy under the Collector that reaches all the way to your village.

Level 1 · District
District Collector
IAS Officer · Most powerful ground-level officer
Wears three hats: Executive head of the district (development), District Magistrate (law & order), and District Election Officer during elections. Oversees all revenue functions and is the final appellate authority for most revenue disputes.
Level 2 · Revenue Division
Revenue Divisional Officer (RDO)
IAS/TN Civil Service · Controls 3–5 taluks
Appeals against Tahsildar orders come here. Oversees land acquisition for government projects. First appellate authority for caste certificate appeals. Also functions as Sub-Divisional Magistrate.
Level 3 · Taluk — The Core Level
Tahsildar
TN Civil Service · Controls a taluk (sub-district)
The workhorse of revenue administration. Issues most certificates, maintains land records, conducts patta transfers, and handles land disputes. If you've ever applied for a government certificate, it came from or through this office.
Caste / Community Certificate Income Certificate Nativity Certificate Patta (land ownership) Legal Heir Certificate OBC/SC/ST Certificate
Level 4 · Sub-Taluk
Deputy Tahsildar / Revenue Inspector
Controls a firka (group of villages)
Field verification officer. When you apply for a certificate, the RI visits your village to verify facts on the ground — income, caste, landholding. Their field report determines your certificate outcome.
Level 5 · Village — First Contact
Village Administrative Officer (VAO)
Controls a revenue village or cluster
The government's presence at the village level. Maintains village accounts (Adangal), knows every land parcel in the village, certifies facts for all revenue documents, and is the first point of contact for any government record. Most government schemes are also verified through the VAO.
Adangal (village land register) Chitta (land classification) Residence/Domicile proof
TN's SMART (Simplified, Moral, Accountable, Responsive, Transparent) initiative digitized most revenue records. You can now access patta, chitta, and adangal online via TN e-District services — but the underlying chain of officers is still what verifies and issues certificates for sensitive applications.
Chapter 10

Watchdogs — Who Checks the Government

Power without accountability becomes corrupt. The Constitution and Parliament created several independent bodies whose entire purpose is to audit, scrutinise, and check what the government does with money and power. They report to the legislature, not to the executive — which is what keeps them independent.

🔍
CAG
Comptroller & Auditor General · Constitutional (Art. 148)
Audits every rupee of government expenditure — Union and State. Cannot be removed by the government (same security of tenure as a Supreme Court judge). Reports directly to Parliament/Legislature, not to any minister.
After audit, reports go to the Public Accounts Committee (PAC) of the Assembly, which cross-examines senior officers about spending lapses.
Famous use: The 2G Spectrum scam (2008) was quantified as a ₹1.76 lakh crore "presumptive loss" by a CAG report — which triggered the Supreme Court cancelling 122 telecom licenses.
📄
RTI
Right to Information Act, 2005 · Citizen's tool
Any citizen can file an RTI application requesting specific information from any public authority. Response must come within 30 days (48 hours if life/liberty is involved).
Escalation path: First Appellate Authority (within the department) → Tamil Nadu State Information Commission → High Court. Central RTI appeals go to the Central Information Commission.
Exemptions: Cabinet notes, national security, personal privacy, ongoing investigations, and information that would harm a third party are exempted. But the burden of proof to claim exemption is on the government, not the citizen.
🏛
Vigilance Commission
TN Vigilance & Anti-Corruption Directorate
Investigates corruption among Tamil Nadu government employees and public servants. Can initiate suo motu investigations without a complaint. Works alongside the Anti-Corruption Branch of TN Police.
At the Union level, the Central Vigilance Commission (CVC) performs a similar role for central government employees and is constitutionally independent.
Key limitation: Cannot investigate sitting Chief Ministers or Cabinet Ministers directly under normal operation — those require either a Governor's reference or a court direction in most scenarios.
⚖️
State Human Rights Commission
SHRC · Tamil Nadu · Statutory Body
Investigates human rights violations by state police, prisons, government institutions. Can summon officers, examine records, and recommend compensation to victims and prosecution of erring officers.
Separate from NHRC (National Human Rights Commission), which handles Union-level violations. The TN Women's Commission is a parallel body for gender-based violations — can investigate complaints, summon accused, and recommend FIRs.
Teeth limitation: Both bodies can only recommend — they cannot compel action. But their reports are admissible as evidence in courts, which is where real enforcement happens.
Chapter 11

Governor's Discretionary Powers & Article 356

The Governor is mostly ceremonial — but at three specific moments, the Governor has genuine discretion that can change political outcomes. Article 356 (President's Rule) is the most extreme intervention possible: the Centre dismissing a state government. Understanding when it's legitimate vs. weaponised is essential.

The Three Discretionary Moments

🗳 Hung Assembly — Who to Invite?
When no single party wins a majority, the Governor must decide who to invite to form government. There's no fixed rule. Conventions: (1) Invite the single largest party, (2) Invite a pre-poll alliance, (3) Ask for proof of majority. If no one can prove majority within a reasonable period → recommend President's Rule.
📜 Stalling Bills — Pocket Veto
No time limit is explicitly set in the Constitution for how long a Governor can sit on a bill. A Governor can simply not sign — a "pocket veto." However, if the Assembly passes the bill a second time, the Governor must give assent. Governors have also "reserved" bills for the President — which can delay for years.
📋 Governor's Report to Centre
The Governor sends periodic reports to the Centre about the state's political situation. An adverse report — saying the state government has broken down constitutionally — is the trigger mechanism for Article 356. This is why Governors and state governments are often in conflict: an adverse report can end a government.
⚡ Floor Test — Governor Can Direct
If there's doubt about whether the CM commands majority, the Governor can direct the CM to prove majority on the floor of the Assembly within a fixed number of days. This happened in TN in 2017 (Palaniswami government) — Governor directed a floor test within 24 hours.

Article 356 — President's Rule, Step by Step

1
Governor sends report to President
States that the government of the state "cannot be carried on in accordance with the provisions of the Constitution." This is the trigger. The report can cite political breakdown, lawlessness, or constitutional crisis.
2
President issues Proclamation
On the advice of the Union Cabinet, the President issues the Art. 356 proclamation. The state government is dismissed. The Governor assumes executive power. The Assembly is suspended (or dissolved).
3
Parliament approves within 2 months
The proclamation must be approved by both Houses of Parliament within 2 months. If Parliament is dissolved during this period, the new Parliament gets 30 days after first sitting. If Parliament does NOT approve — the proclamation lapses and the state government is automatically restored.
4
Duration: max 3 years with extensions
Initial period: 6 months. Can be extended up to 3 years — but each 6-month extension requires fresh Parliament approval. After 1 year, extension requires either a national emergency or Elections Commission certification that elections cannot be held.
5
Bommai Judgment (1994) — The Guardrail
S.R. Bommai v. Union of India is the most important Supreme Court ruling on this. Key holdings: (1) State government must be given opportunity to prove majority on the floor before dismissal. (2) Assembly cannot be dissolved before Parliament approves the proclamation — if Parliament rejects, the government is restored. (3) Art. 356 is justiciable — courts can strike it down if mala fide (misused politically). This judgment dramatically curtailed Art. 356 misuse.
TN's Art. 356 history: Imposed 4 times — 1976 (Emergency), 1980 (after Janata govt dismissals), 1988 (after MGR's death and political instability), 1991 (after Rajiv Gandhi assassination and breakdown of DMK govt). The last use was 33+ years ago — the Bommai guardrail has largely held since.
Soft pressure is more common today: Post-Bommai, outright dismissal is harder. The modern pattern is: Governor withholds assent on bills for months → sends adverse reports → enables Central agencies to operate → creates political instability without technically imposing 356. This is subtler but equally impactful.
Chapter 12

The Budget Cycle — Where Money Comes From and Where It Goes

A budget is the government's annual financial statement. Understanding it requires two things: how India's money flows from Centre to State (via the Finance Commission), and how the state then plans and spends it. These are two distinct but connected systems.

Part A — Union Budget Timeline

Every year, the Finance Minister presents the Union Budget. Here's the cycle that makes it happen:

Oct
Budget circular to all ministries
Finance Ministry asks all central ministries to submit their expenditure demands for the coming year. Each ministry estimates how much it needs.
Nov–Dec
Pre-budget consultations
Finance Minister holds meetings with industry bodies (CII, FICCI), farmers' groups, economists, and state finance ministers to gauge needs and build consensus.
Feb 1
Budget presented in Lok Sabha
Finance Minister presents the budget speech. Two bills tabled: the Finance Bill (tax proposals) and the Appropriation Bill (authorizing spending). This date moved from Feb 28 to Feb 1 in 2017 to give states more time to plan before the financial year starts.
Feb–Mar
Parliamentary debate — Demands for Grants
Parliament debates each ministry's allocation. MPs can move "cut motions" to reduce specific allocations. Practically, the government rarely loses these given majority.
Mar 31
Appropriation Bill + Finance Bill passed
Must be passed before March 31 (end of financial year). The Finance Bill must pass within 75 days of presentation. If the Appropriation Bill isn't ready, Parliament passes a Vote on Account — a temporary authorization allowing government to spend for 2 months while the full budget is finalized.

Follows the same structure as the Union Budget but at the state level. Presented in the Assembly by the State Finance Minister, usually in February or March.

Revenue vs. Capital expenditure: Revenue expenditure is day-to-day spending — salaries, subsidies, interest. Capital expenditure builds assets — roads, buildings, machinery. TN's Finance Commission grievance — the South India problem: Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Karnataka, AP, and Telangana argue the formula penalises them. They controlled population growth (so have fewer people), industrialised (so pay more income tax to Centre), and collect high state taxes — but the largest weight (45%) goes to "income distance," meaning poorer, populous states (UP, Bihar) get proportionally more back. Southern states effectively subsidise the Hindi belt. This is a live and intensifying political tension, not just an academic debate.

Part D — Central Schemes vs. State Schemes

Not all government money works the same way. There are three types of schemes — and understanding the difference explains why states sometimes rename central schemes, and why Centre-State fiscal fights happen.

🇮🇳 Central Sector Schemes
Funded by100% Centre
Designed byCentre
Implemented byCentral agencies, often directly
State roleNone or minimal facilitation
PM Gram Sadak Yojana PM Awas Yojana (Urban) Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan National Pension System
⚖️ Centrally Sponsored Schemes (CSS)
Most politically contentious type
Funded byCentre + State share. For TN: typically 60:40 (Centre:State)
Designed byCentre sets rules & guidelines
Implemented byState government
State roleMust contribute 40%, follow Centre's guidelines, report back
MGNREGS (job guarantee) PM Poshan (midday meal) Jal Jeevan Mission Swachh Bharat Mission PMAY-Gramin (rural housing)
🟠 State Schemes
Funded by100% State
Designed byState — full flexibility
Implemented byState departments
State roleComplete ownership, can start/stop/modify
Kalaignar Magalir Urimai (₹1000/month) Free rice distribution (PDS top-up) CM Breakfast Scheme Free laptops for students Free bus travel for women
The CSS fiscal trap: If the Centre reduces its CSS allocation mid-year (a common budget move), the state has already committed to its 40% share and has often started the project. The state must either cut the scheme (politically painful) or find extra money (fiscally painful). This is why state governments are perpetually in fiscal stress even when they appear to have large budgets — a significant portion is locked into CSS matching obligations.
